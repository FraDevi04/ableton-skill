## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### Start from the Foundation
**Three Ways to Start** (ch01): When facing a blank DAW, choose one: (1) Start with the foundation — bass and drums, the low-end anchor; (2) Start with what you hear — use spontaneous melodic/rhythmic ideas from your head; (3) Start with what you know — use a physical instrument you're comfortable with to generate ideas.

### Extract, Don't Copy
**Catalog of Attributes** (ch02): Listen to inspiring music and write a catalog of its attributes (sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, form). Set aside the original and use only the catalog as a template for new work. This lets you capture the "essence" of inspiration without plagiarism.

### Break Your Own Patterns
**Avoidance List** (ch03): When your music sounds too similar to past work, write a list of specific techniques to avoid on the next track. This forces you into new creative territory and expands your vocabulary.

### Listen Deeply
**Active Listening** (ch04): Listen as the primary activity, not background. Listen in layers (focus on one parameter per pass: sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, form). Listen in chunks (loop 1-2 bars). Listen subjectively (understand why you feel what you feel).

### Prepare the Space
**Mise en Place** (ch05): Before creative sessions, prepare your ingredients (DAWs, samples, hardware), workspace (comfortable, uncluttered), and yourself (no distractions). Make templates for quick starts. Organize as you go. Always be ready to capture ideas.

### Create Constraints
**Arbitrary Constraints** (ch07): Limit your options arbitrarily to avoid decision paralysis. Musical constraints (one sample, no expected instrument), time constraints (deadlines, timeboxing), space constraints (change venue). The more constraints, the more freedom.

### Separate Creation from Editing
**"Write Drunk; Edit Sober"** (ch08): Compartmentalize into two phases. Creation: capture everything, delete nothing, aim for speed and quantity. Editing: ruthlessly curate, trim, reorder. Never mix the two modes — it breaks creative flow.

### Choose Tempo Intentionally
**Choosing a Tempo** (ch09): Genre ranges: Dub 60-90, Hip-hop 60-100, Downtempo 90-120, House 115-130, Techno/Trance 120-140, Dubstep 135-145, Drum & Bass 160-180. Half-time/double-time dichotomy is powerful in bass music. The correct tempo is the one that makes the song work.

### Deep Focus on One Element
**One Part at a Time** (ch10): Instead of sketching broadly, work deeply on one element until release-ready. This develops focus skill, and one great part can inspire others. Treat the single part as the goal, not a building block.

### Change Your Tools
**Different Tools** (ch11): Break routines by using completely different tools — different DAW, plugins, hardware. This forces you out of familiar patterns and into new creative directions. Every tool has unique constraints that open new possibilities.

### Use Presets as Starting Points
**Presets as Starting Points** (ch12): Don't start from scratch or from presets only. Use presets as a departure point, then fine-tune. Commit time to sound design so you're proud of your sounds. A few tweaks (filter, ADSR) can transform any preset.

### Build Your Own Templates
**The Tyranny of the Default** (ch13): Don't let your DAW's defaults dictate your workflow. Build custom template projects with your preferred tempo, instruments, effects, and settings. Save multiple templates for different genres or workflows.

### Write Music Before Sounds
**Simple Sounds** (ch14): Start with generic sounds (General MIDI) and focus on writing strong musical ideas first. Once you have great notes/rhythms, find great sounds to play them. Acoustic instruments help get out of DAW thinking entirely.

### Extended Techniques
**Extended Techniques** (ch15): Use unconventional sound production — prepared piano, bowed metal objects, multiphonics, circuit bending. Bridge the gap between acoustic rawness and electronic flexibility.

### Explore Without Goals
**Goal-less Exploration** (ch16): When uninspired, explore your DAW without pressure to make music. Learn new instruments/effects. Go through sample libraries. Free, non-directed exploration can unlock unexpected creative directions.

### Timebox to Beat Procrastination
**Procrastination and Timeboxing** (ch17): Set fixed short timeboxes (20 min) for tasks. Stop when the timer goes off, even if in flow. This breaks intimidating tasks into manageable chunks and builds momentum.

### Collaborate Strategically
**Collaboration** (ch18): Choose partners for professional compatibility first, social second. Find people who counterbalance your strengths/weaknesses. Define workload, money, and creative rights early.

### Think Like an Amateur
**Thinking Like an Amateur** (ch19): "Amateur" means "lover of." Lower your stress by experimenting in genres where you have no experience. Amateurs are free from outside pressure and often have more pleasurable experiences.

### Reuse Your Scraps
**Scraps and Sketches** (ch20): Treat unfinished projects as a library of musical ideas, not failures. Extract elements for reuse in new tracks. Save even a single 2-bar loop — it might become your next great song.

### Ignore Distraction Problems
**Problem-Solving as Distraction** (ch21): Technical problems during creative time are "Resistance" — your mind's way of avoiding work. Use what you have. Solve problems during non-productive time, not creative time. Music-making time is sacred.

### Develop Your Own Workflow
**Personal Workflow** (ch22): No objective right answer for workflow. Try different methods, keep what works, synthesize your own. Practice until it becomes flow. Answer: What to do? Order? When to move on? When done?

### Quality Over Originality
**Originality vs. Quality** (ch23): Aim for quality, not originality. Originality is a byproduct of quality + varied listening + hard work. If the music is good, that's all that matters.

### Product Over Process
**Process vs. Product** (ch24): Process is scaffolding, not the building. Good process doesn't guarantee good results. Only the final product matters to listeners. Listen critically at every stage and abandon directions that lead to bad results.

### Embrace the Work
**On Work** (ch25): There are no shortcuts. Creating art is fundamentally hard work. Embrace the struggle. Flow states come after painful effort, not before.

### Breadth Before Depth
**Breadth Before Depth** (ch26): In early/middle stages, work broadly — get ideas out quickly before working deeply on one part. Idea generation is fragile and messy; detail work requires a different mindset. Learn to listen for potential, not perfection.

### Think in Three Layers
**Foreground, Middle Ground, Background** (ch27): Divide musical elements into three depth layers. Foreground: high, loud, fast, rapidly changing. Middle ground: moderate activity. Background: steady, consistent. Vary depth temporarily to draw attention.

### Blur Section Boundaries
**Fuzzy Boundaries** (ch28): Make formal section transitions less abrupt by extending/retracting/deleting material in some tracks at boundaries. Use anticipation (drum fills ending early), hesitation (fills extending past boundary), and silence.

### Create Variation Through Mutation
**Mutation of Clones** (ch29): Duplicate an idea 8x, make one meaningful change to each copy. **Mutation Over Generations** (ch30): Make one duplicate, change it, then duplicate the change, and so on. **Note Transformations** (ch31): Transposition, Inversion, Retrograde, Scale constraint, Time shifting, Pitch rotation.

### Find Rhythms in Chaos
**Implied Rhythm in Short Loops** (ch32): Loop tiny fragments (1-2 sec) of any audio. Pareidolia — our brain finds patterns in chaos — will reveal rhythmic ideas. Good sources: field recordings, speech, existing music (against original tempo).

### Polyrhythmic Loops
**Asynchronous or Polyrhythmic Loops** (ch33): Trigger one instrument with multiple clips of different lengths. They create polyrhythmic composites. Add automation loops, LFOs at different rates. Classic "Berlin School" and Brian Eno technique.

### Misuse Your Tools
**Misusing Rhythmic Tools** (ch34): Quantize to wrong note values (generates chords). Slice non-rhythmic material. Feed beat-juggling effects with anything. Learn what tools do, then make them do something else.

### The 3+3+2 Pattern
**3+3+2 Rhythm** (ch35): The tresillo pattern — universal rhythm from African/Latin music through jazz to electronic. Overlay against symmetrical patterns. Vary by skipping/adding notes or extending the "3" portion.

### Program Human Beats
**Programming Beats 1: On Looseness** (ch36): Apply quantization at partial amounts. Quantize only certain instruments. Find your natural tempo. Humanize after quantization. Adjust velocity. Manually shift elements (behind the beat = laid-back, ahead = driving).

### Linear Drumming
**Programming Beats 2: Linear Drumming** (ch37): No two drum instruments play simultaneously — monophonic drum parts. Treat the drum part as a quasi-melodic line. Each instrument equally weighted. Creates "rolling" character at fast tempos.

### Ghost Notes
**Programming Beats 3: Ghost Notes** (ch38): Extremely quiet snare notes between prominent hits. Propel musical time forward. Add quick gestures that anticipate downbeats. Most effective with multisampled drums (velocity-sensitive).

### Drummer Thinking
**Programming Beats 4: Top, Bottom, Left, Right** (ch39): Top = cymbals (timekeeping, simple patterns). Bottom = drums (kick/snare, asymmetrical gestures). Jazz = top-down. Rock = bottom-up. Left/right = hand distribution. Apply physical human restrictions manually for organic feel.

### Kick + Bass as One Line
**Bass Lines and Kick Drums as a Single Composite** (ch40): Don't treat kick and bass as separate lines — compose them as one monophonic composite. Bass notes must fit between kick notes. Zero overlap of attacks. Sustained notes OK if onsets don't overlap.

### Convert Drums to Pitches
**Drums to Pitches and Vice Versa** (ch41): MIDI notes are data independent of sound. Copy drum patterns to pitched instruments (transpose up, constrain to scale). Copy melodic patterns to drums (re-map to appropriate kit). Extract pitch/rhythm from audio using wrong algorithms.

### Drums to Pitches
**Drums to Pitches and Vice Versa** (ch40): MIDI notes are data independent of sound. Copy drum patterns to pitched instruments. Copy melodic patterns to drums.

### Tune Everything
**Tuning Everything** (ch41): Electronic drums often have definite pitches. Tune kick drums to the song key. Tune toms in intervals spelling the root chord. Use spectrum analyzer + EQ to identify and fix out-of-tune percussion.

### Use Silence and Noise
**Silence and Noise** (ch42): Space between notes is part of the texture. Ultra-clean: minimal reverb, percussive elements only. Maximum ambience: record open mic noise, vinyl runout, field recordings with reverb, DAW feedback loops.

### Old-Fashioned Sampling
**Sampling the Old-Fashioned Way** (ch43): Play samples from a sampler (not timeline) so pitch changes alter tempo too. Creates rhythmic variation when transposing — a dotted eighth sample played higher becomes faster. Deliberately use technology limitations.

### Write Melodies by Contour
**Creating Melodies 1: Contour** (ch44): Balance between rise/fall and step/leap motion. Good melodies have a single peak note on a strong beat. Arc contour: start low, climb to middle peak, descend. After step motion, leap in opposite direction (and vice versa).

### Write Melodies from Motives
**Creating Melodies 2: Using Motives** (ch45): Smallest recognizable fragment = motive. Build melodies from 1-2 motives through repetition, variation, and transformation. Identify motives in other music as a listening exercise.

### Counterpoint
**Multiple Simultaneous Melodies** (ch46): Each part must be a standalone melody. Keep rhythms independent (parts move when the other sustains). Use contrary motion (voices move opposite). Be careful with voice crossing. Distinct timbres help maintain independence.

### Hocket in Melodies
**Linear Rhythm in Melodies** (ch47): Notes of a melody alternate between two+ instruments so no two play simultaneously. Fastest at fast tempos with fast-attack sounds. Literal alternation (every other note), or arbitrary groupings. More voices = more possibilities.

### Sound-Color Melody
**Sound-Color Melody** (ch48): Distribute a melody between multiple instruments. Split by pitch range (high voice / low voice). Use instruments with mirrored envelopes (fast attack/gradual decay + slow attack). Smooth, seamless timbral exchange.

### Rhythm of Lyrics
**The Rhythm of Lyrics** (ch49): Scansion — mark syllables as strong (/) or weak (*). Place strong syllables on strong beats, weak on weak. Emphasize stressed syllables with longer duration, higher pitch, or louder volume. Fighting natural text rhythm = strained vocals.

### Harmony Basics
**Creating Harmony 1: The Basics** (ch50): Major scale: W-W-H-W-W-W-H. Minor scale: W-H-W-W-H-W-W. Seven diatonic triads per key. Functional progressions: I→anything, V/vii°→I, ii/IV→V, vi→ii/IV, iii→vi. Most common: I-V-vi-IV.

### Beyond Triads
**Creating Harmony 2: Beyond Triads** (ch52): Seventh chords (dominant 7th wants to resolve to I). 9th, 11th, 13th chords for color. Alterations (b5 = #11). Quartal/quintal chords (stacked 4ths/5ths). Secundal/chord clusters. Voice leading between dense chords.

### Voice Leading
**Voice Leading and Inversions** (ch53): Minimize motion between chords. Preserve common tones. Use inversions to keep voices close. Root position→root position = bad voice leading. Invert to share tones and reduce hand movement.

### Parallel Harmony
**Parallel Harmony** (ch54): Each note moves same semitones in same direction. Essentially transposed copies of one chord. Common in house/deep house. Sampled chords, chord memory synths, chord-generating MIDI tools all create parallel harmony.

### Harmonic Rhythm
**Harmonic Rhythm** (ch55): Rate of chord changes, separate from surface rhythm. Fast surface rhythm → slow harmonic rhythm (one chord per bar). Slow surface → fast harmonic rhythm. Vary harmonic rhythm to create new sections. Speed up at endings.

### Repetition and Insistence
**Repetition and Insistence** (ch56): Angular/atonal patterns sound natural if repeated persistently. Acid/minimal techno uses arbitrary bass lines made musical through repetition. Listen to random patterns loop enough times — they become musical.

### Rhythmic Automation
**The Rhythm of Automation** (ch57): Place automation breakpoints at metric positions for rhythmic envelopes. Instant changes create counterpoint to note events. Real-time recordings work for mix balances; rhythmic envelopes work for creative synth/effect parameters.

### Humanize with Automation
**Humanizing With Automation Envelopes** (ch58): Automate attack/decay for subtle duration changes. Tiny tuning variations (cents) simulate live intonation. Apply to any parameter, not just filter. Randomization can achieve similar effects.

### Maximal Density
**Maximal Density** (ch58): Start with the densest possible section (everything playing). Then subtract elements to create variations. Each density level becomes a song section. Works especially well in minimal techno where sections are similar.

### Deliberately Bad Listening
**Deliberately Bad Listening** (ch59): Listen from corners, other rooms, with headphones off and volume up, or volume extremely low. Hear acoustic illusions, phantom pitches, frequency artifacts. These imperfections can be compositionally useful.

### Randomness and Responsibility
**Randomness and Responsibility** (ch60): Randomization tools lack your taste. You must evaluate every result. Solo, slow down, bounce to audio to assess. Increase randomization until too extreme, then dial back. Listen to and take responsibility for every moment.

### Dramatic Arc
**Dramatic Arc** (ch61): Three-part structure: Exposition (introduce materials) → Rising action (build tension) → Climax (peak tension/drop) → Falling action (release) → Dénouement (return to normal). Works at micro (melody) and macro (DJ set) scales.

### Arrange by Subtraction
**Arranging as a Subtractive Process** (ch62): Fill entire timeline with material in 20 seconds. Then sculpt by removing, not adding. Easier to hear what's bad than to imagine what's good. Work backwards from end if genre calls for it.

### Erase Ruthlessly
**The Power of Erasing** (ch63): If music feels weak, take parts away rather than adding. Each part requires others to fight for attention. Thin out elements (fewer notes, shorter decays). Add parts only as necessary.

### Copy Song Structures
**Formal Skeletons** (ch64): Load a reference song into your DAW. Mark all formal divisions. Delete the audio, keep markers as arrangement skeleton. Arrange your own material into this proven structure. Trust instincts if materials don't fit perfectly.

### Song Form Elements
**Common Forms 1: Elements of Song Form** (ch65): Verse (main body, 16-32 bars), Chorus (contrast, hook, resolution), Bridge (contrast, different key/chords, once only). Common form: ABABCB (Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus-Bridge-Chorus).

### Layering as Form
**Common Forms 2: Layering as Form** (ch66): Pure electronic genres use textural density variation instead of sectional contrast. Buildup (add layers, increase energy), Breakdown (remove layers, create space), Drop (peak density). Bass line or drum pattern may persist throughout.

### Short Loop Endings
**Short Loops as Endings** (ch67): Isolate a 1-2 bar sub-passage from the closing phrase. Repeat it a set number of times. Optionally shorten further (2 bars → 1 bar). Creates illusion of speeding up toward the end. Add unique events for drive.

### One-Time Events
**Unique Events** (ch68): Insert sounds/gestures/processing that occur only once. Single events (unusual samples), single musical gestures (one-off pattern variations), single processing gestures (one-time effects activation). Use sparingly — too many creates predictability.

### Three Ways to End
**Three Ways to End** (ch69): (1) Mirror what you did at the start — reverse the accumulation. (2) Loop and add/subtract layers at each repeat. (3) Fade out — only if closing material would work looping indefinitely.

### Render Early
**Rendering as Commitment** (ch70): Render to audio early to eliminate ability to tweak. Forces forward movement. Streamlines collaboration. Reduces CPU load. Can also render effects processing for creative experimentation. Save versions pre/post rendering.

### Get Feedback Wisely
**Getting Feedback** (ch71): Ask for honest criticism, not praise. Best sources: other producers or serious fans in your genre. Internet forums > anonymous sites. Ask for specifics, not "do you like it?" Quick mastering preset helps evaluate unfinished tracks fairly.

### Recognize Diminishing Returns
**Diminishing Returns** (ch72): Learn to recognize when "good enough" has been reached. Late-stage tweaking often makes things worse. Fear of commitment drives endless refinement. Stop when continuing yields meaningless results. Finish more music without sacrificing quality.

### Finish Bad Songs Too
**Fail Better** (ch74): Each stage of music-making requires practice — including finishing. More unfinished tracks = fewer finishing opportunities. Force yourself to finish even bad tracks. Get practice failing. Return to Scraps and Sketches for material reuse.
