# Glossary — Making Music

Key terms from Dennis DeSantis's *Making Music: 74 Creative Strategies for Electronic Music Producers*.

## A
- **Active Listening**: Listening as the PRIMARY activity, not background. Multiple passes focused on different parameters (sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, form).
- **Amateur**: "Lover of." Someone who creates for pleasure rather than professional pressure. Amateur thinking lowers stress and increases creativity.
- **Anticipation**: A fuzzy boundary technique where a drum fill or transition element ends early, creating a "head start" for the next section.
- **Arbitrary Constraints**: Self-imposed limitations that eliminate valid options to avoid decision paralysis. The constraints are truly arbitrary — you're eliminating good options.

## B
- **Breadth Before Depth**: Work broadly first — get ideas out quickly. Then work deeply on promising directions. Idea generation and detail work require different mindsets.
- **Breakdown**: In electronic music, the section where layers are removed and energy decreases. Part of the buildup/breakdown/drop form.

## C
- **Catalog of Attributes**: A structured description of an inspiring piece's sound, harmony, melody, rhythm, and form. Write it down, set aside the original, and create new music using only the catalog as a guide.
- **Chorus**: The contrast section in song form. Contains the hook (most memorable part). Provides resolution and emotional release.
- **Common Form (ABABCB)**: Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus-Bridge-Chorus. The most common song form in popular music.
- **Common Tones**: Notes shared between consecutive chords. Preserving common tones in the same voice is a core voice leading technique.
- **Composite (Kick + Bass)**: Treating kick drum and bass line as a single monophonic line. Bass notes fit between kick notes. Zero overlap of attacks.
- **Contrary Motion**: Voices moving in opposite directions (one goes up, the other goes down). Maximizes independence in polyphonic textures.

## D
- **Dénouement**: The resolution phase of the dramatic arc. Return to normal after the climax and falling action.
- **Diatonic Triads**: Seven triads built on each scale degree. In major: I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii°.
- **Diminishing Returns**: The point where additional tweaking yields progressively smaller improvements, then becomes counterproductive.
- **Dramatic Arc**: Three-part structure: Exposition → Rising action → Climax → Falling action → Dénouement. Works at micro, meso, and macro scales.

## E
- **Extended Techniques**: Unconventional sound production methods — prepared piano, bowed metal objects, multiphonics, circuit bending. Bridges acoustic rawness and electronic flexibility.
- **Avoidance List**: A list of specific techniques to avoid on the next track. Forces new creative territory and expands vocabulary.

## F
- **Foreground, Middle Ground, Background**: Three-layer depth model. Foreground = high, loud, fast, rapidly changing. Background = steady, consistent.
- **Functional Progressions**: Chord progressions based on chord function: I→anything, V→I, ii/IV→V, vi→ii/IV, iii→vi.
- **Fuzzy Boundaries**: Making section transitions less abrupt by extending, retracting, or deleting material at boundaries.

## G
- **Ghost Notes**: Extremely quiet snare notes between prominent hits. Propel musical time forward. Most effective with multisampled drums.

## H
- **Harmonic Rhythm**: The rate at which chords change, separate from surface rhythm.
- **Hocket**: Notes of a melody alternate between two+ instruments so no two play simultaneously. Creates a fragmented but unified melody.

## I
- **Implied Rhythm**: Rhythmic patterns revealed by looping tiny fragments of any audio. Our brain's pareidolia finds patterns in chaos.
- **Inversion**: Flipping the contour of a melody — if the original goes up, the inversion goes down by the same amount.
- **Avoidance List**: A pre-planned list of techniques to avoid, forcing new creative directions.

## L
- **Linear Drumming**: No two drum instruments play simultaneously. Monophonic drum parts treated as quasi-melodic lines.
- **Linear Rhythm in Melodies**: Same concept as linear drumming but applied to melodic instruments (hocket).
- **Loop Threshold**: The number of repetitions after which a pattern starts to sound musical.

## M
- **Mise en Place**: From culinary world — prepare tools and ingredients before cooking. Applied to music: set up everything needed before the creative impulse.
- **Motive**: The smallest recognizable fragment of music. Usually 2-4 notes. The DNA of a melody.
- **Monophonic Composite**: Kick and bass as a single line — bass notes fit between kick notes, zero overlap of attacks.
- **Mutation of Clones**: Duplicate an idea 8 times, make one meaningful change to each copy.
- **Mutation Over Generations**: Make one duplicate, change it, then duplicate the change, and so on.

## O
- **Old-Fashioned Sampling**: Playing samples from a sampler (keyboard mode) so pitch changes alter tempo too. Creates rhythmic variation through transposition.

## P
- **Pareidolia**: The brain's tendency to find patterns in chaos. Applied to listening: looping tiny audio fragments reveals rhythmic ideas.
- **Parallel Harmony**: Each note moves the same number of semitones in the same direction. Essentially transposed copies of one chord.
- **Partial Quantization**: Applying quantization at partial amounts (e.g., 50%). Not all-or-nothing.
- **Polyrhythmic Composites**: One instrument triggered by multiple clips of different lengths, creating complex evolving patterns.
- **Presets as Starting Points**: Use presets as a departure point, then fine-tune. Maximizes productivity while maintaining ownership.

## Q
- **Quartal Chords**: Chords built by stacking 4ths instead of 3rds. Creates open, ambiguous harmony.
- **Quintal Chords**: Chords built by stacking 5ths. Even more open than quartal.

## R
- **Resistance**: Technical problems that arise during creative time are often your mind's way of avoiding creative work.
- **Retrograde**: Playing notes in reverse order. Creates a completely different rhythmic/melodic feel.

## S
- **Scansion**: Marking syllables as strong (/) or weak (*). The poetic analysis of rhythm in text.
- **Scraps and Sketches**: Unfinished projects treated as a library of reusable material, not failures.
- **Simple Sounds**: Using generic sounds (General MIDI) to focus purely on writing music, not sound design.
- **Subtractive Arranging**: Fill timeline with material, then sculpt by removing, not adding.
- **Sound-Color Melody (Klangfarbenmelodie)**: Distributing a melody between multiple instruments. The melody changes timbre as it progresses.

## T
- **Tresillo (3+3+2)**: The most universal rhythm in Western music. Three groups of pulses: 3+3+2.
- **Tuning Everything**: Recognizing that electronic drums have definite pitches and tuning them to the song key.

## V
- **Voice Leading**: Smooth movement of individual voices between chords. Minimize motion, preserve common tones, use inversions.

## W
- **Write Drunk; Edit Sober**: Compartmentalize into two phases — creation (capture everything) and editing (ruthlessly curate). Never mix the two modes.
