# Chapter 13: Simple Sounds

## Core Idea
Start with the simplest sounds available (General MIDI, acoustic instruments) and focus on writing strong musical ideas first. Find great sounds AFTER you have great notes.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Simple sounds first**: Use generic sounds (General MIDI) to remove sound-design thinking and focus purely on music. Work on notes and rhythms without tweaking sounds or adding effects.
- **Music-first philosophy**: Strong musical ideas transcend weak sounds. If an idea is good enough to work with Generic MIDI, it'll work with anything.
- **Acoustic instrument bypass**: Playing guitar or piano gets you out of DAW thinking entirely — write with your ears, not your eyes.

## Key Concepts
- **Sound design distraction**: Collecting perfect sounds before writing music loses the musical idea
- **Transcendent ideas**: Great melodies/rhythms work regardless of sound quality
- **Composer mindset**: Symphonies written at piano prove that ideas matter more than sounds
- **Recording acoustic play**: Set up a mic to record acoustic playing, then transcribe later

## Mental Models
- Use simple sounds when you're getting lost in sound design instead of writing music
- Think of simple sounds as a "truth test" — if it works with Generic MIDI, it's a good idea
- Acoustic instruments remove both sound-design thinking AND DAW thinking

## Anti-patterns
- **Collecting sounds before writing**: Losing the musical idea while tweaking presets
- **Sound design paralysis**: Never finishing any musical idea because you're always tweaking sounds
- **Ignoring acoustic alternatives**: Staying in the DAW when a physical instrument would help

## Key Takeaways
1. Use generic sounds (General MIDI) to focus purely on writing music
2. If an idea works with simple sounds, it'll work with anything
3. Strong musical ideas transcend weak sounds
4. Acoustic instruments remove both sound-design thinking AND DAW thinking
5. Record acoustic playing with a mic, transcribe later
