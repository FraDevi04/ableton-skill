# Chapter 45: Creating Melodies 2: Using Motives

## Core Idea
The smallest recognizable fragment of music = motive. Build melodies from 1-2 motives through repetition, variation, and transformation. Identify motives in other music as a listening exercise.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Motive**: The smallest recognizable fragment of music. Usually 2-4 notes. The DNA of a melody.
- **Motive building**: Build melodies from 1-2 motives through repetition (repeat the motive), variation (change the motive slightly), and transformation (change the motive significantly).
- **Listening exercise**: Identify motives in other music — this trains your ear to recognize what makes a motive recognizable.

## Key Concepts
- **Small DNA**: Motives are tiny — 2-4 notes — but they carry the identity of a melody
- **Repetition**: The motive must be recognizable through repetition
- **Variation**: The motive can change slightly while remaining recognizable
- **Transformation**: The motive can change significantly while still being traceable to the original

## Mental Models
- Use motive building when your melodies feel shapeless or forgettable
- Think of motives as "musical DNA" — small but identity-defining
- Build melodies by repeating, varying, and transforming motives

## Anti-patterns
- **Too many motives**: Using too many motives creates a melody without identity
- **No repetition**: If the motive never repeats, it's not a motive — it's just a fragment
- **No variation**: Repeating the motive exactly creates boredom

## Key Takeaways
1. A motive is the smallest recognizable fragment of music — usually 2-4 notes
2. Build melodies from 1-2 motives through repetition, variation, and transformation
3. Identify motives in other music as a listening exercise
4. The motive must be recognizable through repetition
5. Variation keeps the motive fresh; transformation creates development
