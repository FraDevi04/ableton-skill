# Chapter 43: Sampling the Old-Fashioned Way

## Core Idea
Play samples from a sampler (not timeline) so pitch changes alter tempo too. Creates rhythmic variation when transposing — a dotted eighth sample played higher becomes faster. Deliberately use technology limitations.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Old-fashioned sampling**: Play samples from a sampler (keyboard mode) rather than placing them on a timeline. When you change pitch, the tempo changes too — this creates rhythmic variation.
- **Pitch-tempo link**: A dotted eighth sample played a fifth higher plays faster (because the pitch change shortens the sample duration). This creates natural rhythmic variation.
- **Technology limitations as features**: Deliberately use the limitations of older sampling technology for creative effect.

## Key Concepts
- **Sampler vs. timeline**: Timeline placement = fixed tempo. Sampler playback = pitch-linked tempo.
- **Rhythmic variation through transposition**: Different pitches = different tempos = rhythmic variation
- **Dotted rhythms**: Dotted eighth samples create particularly interesting rhythmic variations when transposed
- **Intentional limitation**: Using sampler pitch-tempo coupling is a deliberate creative choice

## Mental Models
- Use old-fashioned sampling when you need rhythmic variation from a single sample
- Think of pitch as a tempo control — higher pitch = faster playback
- Embrace technology limitations as creative tools

## Anti-patterns
- **Timeline-only sampling**: Always placing samples on the timeline loses the pitch-tempo link
- **Time-stretching**: Using time-stretch to keep tempo constant defeats the creative purpose
- **Ignoring the variation**: Not listening for the rhythmic possibilities of transposed samples

## Key Takeaways
1. Play samples from a sampler (keyboard mode) — pitch changes alter tempo
2. A dotted eighth sample played higher becomes faster — creating rhythmic variation
3. This is a deliberate use of technology limitations for creative effect
4. Different pitches = different tempos = natural rhythmic variation
5. Time-stretching is the enemy of this technique — embrace the pitch-tempo link
