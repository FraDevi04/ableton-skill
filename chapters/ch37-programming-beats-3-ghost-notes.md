# Chapter 37: Programming Beats 3: Ghost Notes

## Core Idea
Extremely quiet snare notes between prominent hits. Propel musical time forward. Add quick gestures that anticipate downbeats. Most effective with multisampled drums.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Ghost notes**: Extremely quiet snare drum notes placed between prominent hits. They're barely audible but propel the music forward.
- **Anticipation**: Ghost notes often anticipate downbeats — they create a sense of forward motion by hinting at what's coming.
- **Multisampled drums**: Ghost notes are most effective with multisampled drums (velocity-sensitive samples) — the quiet velocity triggers different samples.

## Key Concepts
- **Barely audible**: Ghost notes should be barely audible — if you can clearly hear them, they're not ghost notes
- **Forward propulsion**: Ghost notes create a sense of forward motion
- **Velocity sensitivity**: Multisampled drums respond to velocity with different samples — use low velocity for ghost notes
- **Rhythmic glue**: Ghost notes connect prominent hits, making the beat feel more cohesive

## Mental Models
- Use ghost notes when you want beats that feel more alive and propulsive
- Think of ghost notes as "musical breath" — they give the beat room to move
- Place ghost notes to anticipate downbeats

## Anti-patterns
- **Too loud**: If ghost notes are clearly audible, they're not ghost notes — they're just quiet notes
- **Too many**: Too many ghost notes create confusion
- **Wrong placement**: Ghost notes should anticipate downbeats, not random positions

## Key Takeaways
1. Ghost notes are extremely quiet snare notes between prominent hits
2. They propel musical time forward — barely audible but felt
3. Place ghost notes to anticipate downbeats
4. Most effective with multisampled drums (velocity-sensitive samples)
5. Ghost notes are the "secret sauce" of human-feeling beats
