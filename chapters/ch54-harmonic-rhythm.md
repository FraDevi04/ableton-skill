# Chapter 54: Harmonic Rhythm

## Core Idea
Rate of chord changes, separate from surface rhythm. Fast surface rhythm → slow harmonic rhythm (one chord per bar). Slow surface → fast harmonic rhythm. Vary harmonic rhythm to create new sections.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Harmonic rhythm**: The rate at which chords change. Separate from surface rhythm (the rhythm of individual notes).
- **Inverse relationship**: Fast surface rhythm often pairs with slow harmonic rhythm (one chord per bar). Slow surface rhythm can support fast harmonic rhythm.
- **Section creation**: Varying harmonic rhythm is one way to create new sections — speeding up harmonic rhythm at endings creates forward momentum.

## Key Concepts
- **Two rhythms**: Surface rhythm (note events) and harmonic rhythm (chord changes) are independent
- **Section contrast**: Changing harmonic rhythm creates section contrast without changing melody or timbre
- **Speeding up at endings**: Accelerating harmonic rhythm at the end of a section creates forward momentum
- **Harmonic rhythm as arrangement tool**: Changing how often chords change is a powerful arrangement technique

## Mental Models
- Use harmonic rhythm thinking when sections feel too similar
- Think of harmonic rhythm as a separate dimension of musical variation
- Speed up harmonic rhythm at endings to create momentum

## Anti-patterns
- **Same harmonic rhythm throughout**: Not varying chord change rate creates monotony
- **Ignoring the inverse relationship**: Fast surface rhythm with fast harmonic rhythm creates harmonic confusion
- **Random changes**: Changing harmonic rhythm without musical purpose

## Key Takeaways
1. Harmonic rhythm = rate of chord changes, separate from surface rhythm
2. Fast surface rhythm → slow harmonic rhythm (one chord per bar)
3. Vary harmonic rhythm to create new sections
4. Speed up harmonic rhythm at endings to create forward momentum
5. Harmonic rhythm is an arrangement tool — use it to create section contrast
