# Chapter 44: Creating Melodies 1: Contour

## Core Idea
Balance between rise/fall and step/leap motion. Good melodies have a single peak note on a strong beat. Arc contour: start low, climb to middle peak, descend. After step motion, leap in opposite direction.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Contour balance**: Balance between rise/fall (direction) and step/leap (distance between notes). Good melodies have both upward and downward motion.
- **Single peak principle**: Good melodies have a single peak note — the highest point — placed on a strong beat.
- **Arc contour**: Start low, climb to a middle peak, then descend. The most common melodic contour.
- **Step/leap alternation**: After step motion (moving to adjacent scale degree), leap in the opposite direction (and vice versa).

## Key Concepts
- **Direction**: Melodies should have both rising and falling motion
- **Distance**: The distance between consecutive notes (step = adjacent scale degree, leap = non-adjacent)
- **Peak placement**: The highest note should be on a strong beat for maximum impact
- **Arc shape**: The most natural melodic contour is an arc — low to high to low

## Mental Models
- Use contour thinking when writing melodies that feel directionless
- Think of melodies as shapes — the arc is the most natural shape
- Balance step and leap motion for variety

## Anti-patterns
- **No peak**: Melodies that never reach a clear peak feel directionless
- **Multiple peaks**: Too many peaks create confusion about the melodic center
- **Only steps or only leaps**: Purely stepwise or purely leaping melodies are boring

## Key Takeaways
1. Balance rise/fall and step/leap motion in your melodies
2. Good melodies have a single peak note on a strong beat
3. Arc contour (start low, climb to middle peak, descend) is the most common
4. After step motion, leap in the opposite direction (and vice versa)
5. Think of melodies as shapes — the arc is the most natural shape
