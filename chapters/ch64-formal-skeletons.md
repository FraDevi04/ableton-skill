# Chapter 64: Formal Skeletons

## Core Idea
Load a reference song into your DAW. Mark all formal divisions. Delete the audio, keep markers as arrangement skeleton. Arrange your own material into this proven structure. Trust instincts if materials don't fit perfectly.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Formal skeleton**: Load a reference song into your DAW. Mark all formal divisions (verse, chorus, bridge, etc.). Delete the audio, keep the markers. Use markers as an arrangement skeleton for your own material.
- **Proven structure**: The reference song's structure has already been tested by listeners — use it as a proven framework.
- **Instinct over markers**: If your materials don't fit the markers perfectly, trust your instincts and adjust.

## Key Concepts
- **Reference as template**: A reference song provides a structural template, not a musical template
- **Markers as guide**: Markers show WHERE sections change, not WHAT should be in each section
- **Flexibility**: The skeleton is a guide — adjust it to fit your material
- **Proven vs. original**: The structure is borrowed; the material is original

## Mental Models
- Use formal skeletons when you're unsure about arrangement structure
- Think of it as "standing on the shoulders of giants" — use proven structures
- Trust your instincts when the skeleton doesn't fit

## Anti-patterns
- **Copying the reference**: Using the reference's sounds or melodies defeats the purpose
- **Rigid adherence**: Forcing your material into markers that don't fit
- **Ignoring the reference**: Not using the skeleton at all when you need structure

## Key Takeaways
1. Load a reference song into your DAW, mark all formal divisions
2. Delete the audio, keep markers as arrangement skeleton
3. Arrange your own material into this proven structure
4. Trust your instincts if materials don't fit the markers perfectly
5. The structure is borrowed; the material is original
