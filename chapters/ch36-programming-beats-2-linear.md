# Chapter 36: Programming Beats 2: Linear Drumming

## Core Idea
No two drum instruments play simultaneously — monophonic drum parts. Treat the drum part as a quasi-melodic line. Each instrument is equally weighted, creating a "rolling" character at fast tempos.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Linear drumming**: No two drum instruments play at the same time. The drum part is monophonic — each hit uses a different drum instrument.
- **Quasi-melodic**: Treat the drum part as a melodic line — each drum has a "pitch" (timbre), and the sequence of hits creates a melody of timbres.
- **Equal weighting**: Each drum instrument is equally weighted — no instrument is inherently "more important" than another.

## Key Concepts
- **Monophonic drums**: Like a monophonic synthesizer, only one voice at a time
- **Timbre melody**: The sequence of different drum sounds creates a melodic pattern
- **Rolling character**: At fast tempos, linear drumming creates a smooth, rolling feel
- **Cross-stick/rim shot**: These sounds add timbral variety to linear parts

## Mental Models
- Use linear drumming when you want drum parts that feel melodic and flowing
- Think of drums as a single instrument with multiple timbres
- Each drum hit is a note in a timbre melody

## Anti-patterns
- **Simultaneous hits**: The definition of linear druming is NO simultaneous hits — breaking this defeats the purpose
- **Unequal weighting**: Treating some drums as "more important" breaks the linear approach
- **Too slow**: Linear drumming works best at fast tempos — at slow tempos it can feel sparse

## Key Takeaways
1. No two drum instruments play simultaneously — the part is monophonic
2. Treat the drum part as a quasi-melodic line of timbres
3. Each drum instrument is equally weighted
4. Linear drumming creates a "rolling" character at fast tempos
5. This approach creates drum parts that feel more melodic and less repetitive
