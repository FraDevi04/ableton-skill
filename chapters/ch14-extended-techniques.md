# Chapter 14: Extended Techniques

## Core Idea
Use unconventional sound production methods to bridge the gap between acoustic rawness and electronic flexibility — prepared instruments, circuit bending, and other "improper" techniques.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Extended Techniques**: Unconventional sound production methods from classical music (prepared piano, bowed metal objects, multiphonics on wind instruments) applied to electronic music.
- **Acoustic-to-electronic bridge**: Acoustic instruments offer unique rawness; electronic instruments offer unique flexibility. Extended techniques combine both.
- **Circuit bending**: Rewiring toys/electronics for unintended sounds. "The best way to learn about sound is to break things."

## Key Concepts
- **Improper use**: Playing a piano "wrong" (inserting objects between strings) creates new sounds
- **Rawness vs. flexibility**: Acoustic = raw and unpredictable; electronic = flexible and precise
- **Circuit bending as learning**: Breaking things teaches you how they work
- **Historical precedent**: Extended techniques have a long history in classical/contemporary music

## Mental Models
- Use extended techniques when you want sounds that feel both organic and electronic
- Think of instruments as having more possibilities than their "proper" use reveals
- Breaking things is a valid form of learning

## Anti-patterns
- **Destroying expensive equipment**: Circuit bending should be done on cheap/old gear
- **Forcing the technique**: If an extended technique doesn't serve the music, don't use it
- **Ignoring the sound quality**: Extended techniques can produce terrible sounds — curate carefully

## Key Takeaways
1. Extended techniques create sounds that bridge acoustic rawness and electronic flexibility
2. Prepare pianos (objects between strings), bow metal objects, explore multiphonics
3. Circuit bending teaches you about sound through experimentation and failure
4. Historical precedent exists — extended techniques are legitimate compositional tools
5. Always curate the results — not every extended technique produces musical output
