# Chapter 72: Diminishing Returns

## Core Idea
Learn to recognize when "good enough" has been reached. Late-stage tweaking often makes things worse. Fear of commitment drives endless refinement. Stop when continuing yields meaningless results.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Diminishing returns**: At some point, additional tweaking yields progressively smaller improvements. Beyond a certain point, tweaking makes things WORSE.
- **Late-stage degradation**: Late-stage tweaking often makes things worse — you're not improving, you're degrading.
- **Fear of commitment**: Endless refinement is often driven by fear of commitment — fear that the music isn't "good enough."
- **Good enough**: Learn to recognize when "good enough" has been reached. This is a skill that improves with practice.

## Key Concepts
- **The拐点 (inflection point)**: There's a point where tweaking stops helping and starts hurting
- **Commitment fear**: The fear of releasing imperfect music drives endless refinement
- **Skill development**: Recognizing "good enough" is a skill that improves with practice
- **More tracks > perfect tracks**: Finishing more music at "good enough" quality is better than finishing fewer tracks at "perfect" quality

## Mental Models
- Use diminishing returns thinking when you've been working on a track for too long
- Think of "good enough" as a target, not a compromise
- More finished tracks = more practice = better skills

## Anti-patterns
- **Endless tweaking**: Tweak, tweak, tweak — never finishing
- **Perfect is the enemy of good**: Waiting for perfection means nothing gets finished
- **Ignoring the拐点**: Not recognizing when tweaking has become counterproductive

## Key Takeaways
1. Learn to recognize when "good enough" has been reached
2. Late-stage tweaking often makes things worse
3. Fear of commitment drives endless refinement — recognize this fear
4. Stop when continuing yields meaningless results
5. More finished tracks at "good enough" quality > fewer tracks at "perfect" quality
