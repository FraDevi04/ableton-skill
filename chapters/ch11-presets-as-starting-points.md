# Chapter 11: Presets as Starting Points

## Core Idea
Use presets, samples, and loops as a departure point, then fine-tune them to your taste. This maximizes productivity while maintaining ownership of your sound.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Preset-as-departure**: Start with a preset in the right family (bass preset for bass line), work on melody/rhythm, then come back to fine-tune the sound.
- **The middle ground**: Between purists (erase all presets) and loop-assemblers (no sound design at all). Use presets as starting points with the implicit understanding that you'll devote time to fine-tuning.
- **Six-control sound design**: Filter + ADSR (attack, decay, sustain, release) = 6 controls that can create huge ranges of variation from any preset.

## Key Concepts
- **Purist trap**: Starting from default presets is time-consuming and context-blind
- **Guilt-free presets**: Exploring bass presets for a bass line is not cheating
- **Fine-tuning ownership**: A few tweaks transform any preset into something uniquely yours
- **Basic synth theory**: Understanding filter and envelope basics applies across all synthesizers

## Mental Models
- Use presets when you need to move quickly from idea to sound
- Commit at least some time to sound design so you're proud of your sounds
- Learn the 6 basic controls (filter, ADSR) — they work on almost any synth

## Anti-patterns
- **Erasing all presets**: Unnecessary restriction that ignores musical context
- **Using presets without tweaking**: Never making them your own
- **No sound design experience at all**: Using presets without ever learning to modify them

## Key Takeaways
1. Use presets guilt-free — they're a starting point, not a crutch
2. Find a preset close to what you imagine, then work on musical parameters
3. Come back to sound design after you have music underway
4. Learn basic synth theory (filter, ADSR) — 6 controls can transform any preset
5. Commit enough time to sound design so you're proud of your sounds
