# Chapter 52: Voice Leading and Inversions

## Core Idea
Minimize motion between chords. Preserve common tones. Use inversions to keep voices close. Root position→root position = bad voice leading. Invert to share tones and reduce hand movement.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Voice leading**: The smooth movement of individual voices between chords. Minimize motion — each voice moves the shortest distance possible.
- **Common tones**: Chords that share notes should preserve those shared notes in the same voice.
- **Inversions**: Changing which note is the bass note. Root position→root position = bad voice leading. Invert to share tones and reduce motion.
- **Close position**: Keep voices as close together as possible within the constraints of voice leading.

## Key Concepts
- **Motion minimization**: Each voice should move as little as possible between chords
- **Common tone preservation**: Shared notes stay in the same voice
- **Inversion as tool**: Inversions are not just for bass lines — they're voice-leading tools
- **Root position trap**: Moving from root position to root position forces all voices to jump

## Mental Models
- Use voice leading when chord progressions feel jumpy or disconnected
- Think of each voice as an independent line that should flow smoothly
- Inversions are your primary tool for good voice leading

## Anti-patterns
- **Root position chain**: Moving root position chords one after another = bad voice leading
- **Ignoring common tones**: Not preserving shared notes between chords
- **Wide voice spacing**: Spreading voices too far apart creates gaps

## Key Takeaways
1. Minimize motion between chords — each voice moves the shortest distance
2. Preserve common tones in the same voice
3. Use inversions to keep voices close and share tones
4. Root position→root position = bad voice leading
5. Invert to share tones and reduce hand movement
