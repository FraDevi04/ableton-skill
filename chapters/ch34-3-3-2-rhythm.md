# Chapter 34: 3+3+2 Rhythm

## Core Idea
The tresillo pattern — a universal rhythm found from African music through jazz to electronic. Overlay against symmetrical patterns. Vary by skipping/adding notes or extending the "3" portion.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **3+3+2 (tresillo)**: The most universal rhythm in Western music. Three groups of pulses: 3+3+2 (or 3+3+2+3+3+2 for an octave). Found in African music, Latin music, jazz, blues, rock, and electronic music.
- **Overlay technique**: Play the 3+3+2 pattern against symmetrical patterns (4/4, 2/4). The asymmetry creates tension and interest.
- **Variation techniques**: Skip notes, add notes, or extend the "3" portion to create variations on the basic pattern.

## Key Concepts
- **Universal rhythm**: The 3+3+2 pattern appears across cultures and genres
- **Asymmetry**: The uneven grouping (3+3+2) creates rhythmic tension against symmetrical patterns
- **Syncopation**: The pattern is inherently syncopated — it emphasizes off-beats
- **Cross-cultural**: The pattern connects African, Latin, jazz, and electronic music traditions

## Mental Models
- Use the 3+3+2 pattern when you need a rhythm that feels both familiar and interesting
- Think of it as a "rhythm cheat code" — it works in almost any context
- Overlay against symmetrical patterns for maximum tension

## Anti-patterns
- **Overusing the pattern**: 3+3+2 is ubiquitous — using it everywhere makes your music sound generic
- **Ignoring variations**: The basic pattern is well-known — vary it to keep it fresh
- **Forcing it**: Not every rhythm needs a 3+3+2 pattern — use it when it serves the music

## Key Takeaways
1. The 3+3+2 (tresillo) pattern is universal — found in African, Latin, jazz, and electronic music
2. Overlay it against symmetrical patterns for rhythmic tension
3. Vary the pattern by skipping/adding notes or extending the "3" portion
4. The pattern is inherently syncopated — it emphasizes off-beats
5. Use it as a starting point, not a crutch — vary it to keep it fresh
