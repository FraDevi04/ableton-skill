# Chapter 47: Linear Rhythm in Melodies

## Core Idea
Notes of a melody alternate between two+ instruments so no two play simultaneously. Fastest at fast tempos with fast-attack sounds. Literal alternation (every other note), or arbitrary groupings.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Hocket**: Notes of a melody alternate between two or more instruments so no two play simultaneously. Each instrument plays a fragment of the melody, and the fragments combine to form the complete melody.
- **Literal alternation**: Every other note alternates between instruments (instrument A plays notes 1, 3, 5; instrument B plays notes 2, 4, 6).
- **Arbitrary groupings**: Notes are grouped arbitrarily (instrument A plays groups of 3, instrument B plays groups of 2).
- **Tempo dependence**: Hocket is fastest and most effective at fast tempos with fast-attack sounds.

## Key Concepts
- **Fragmented melody**: The melody is split between instruments — no single instrument plays the whole thing
- **Combination**: The listener's brain combines the fragments into a single perceived melody
- **Fast attack required**: Hocket requires fast-attack sounds — slow attack sounds blur the alternation
- **More voices = more possibilities**: Adding more instruments creates more complex hocket patterns

## Mental Models
- Use hocket when you want a melody that feels distributed across instruments
- Think of it as "splitting the melody" — each instrument plays a fragment
- Fast tempos + fast attack = best results

## Anti-patterns
- **Slow attack sounds**: Slow attack sounds blur the alternation and defeat the purpose
- **Too slow**: Hocket at slow tempos can feel disjointed
- **Too many voices**: Adding too many voices creates confusion

## Key Takeaways
1. Hocket: notes of a melody alternate between two+ instruments
2. No two instruments play simultaneously — each plays a fragment
3. Literal alternation (every other note) or arbitrary groupings
4. Fastest at fast tempos with fast-attack sounds
5. More voices = more possibilities, but also more complexity
