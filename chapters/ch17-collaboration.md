# Chapter 17: Collaboration

## Core Idea
Choose collaborators for professional compatibility first, social compatibility second. Define workload, money, and creative rights early to avoid conflicts later.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Professional compatibility first**: Choose collaborators based on work style, reliability, and complementary skills — not friendship. Social compatibility can develop later.
- **Role division**: Define who does what early. Common splits: one handles music, one handles sound design; or one handles production, one handles melody.
- **Money and credit**: Discuss financial arrangements and credit allocation BEFORE starting.

## Key Concepts
- **Complementary skills**: The best collaborators have skills you lack
- **Work style matching**: A meticulous producer needs a meticulous collaborator, not a chaotic one
- **Conflict prevention**: Early discussion of money, credit, and roles prevents later conflicts
- **Collaboration as learning**: Working with someone exposes you to different approaches

## Mental Models
- Use collaboration when your skills are complemented by another person
- Think of collaboration as a professional partnership first, friendship second
- Define the rules of engagement before starting — it's not about distrust, it's about clarity

## Anti-patterns
- **Collaborating with friends only**: Friendship doesn't guarantee professional compatibility
- **Deferring difficult conversations**: Not discussing money/credit until after a song is finished
- **Unequal workload without discussion**: Assuming equal effort when contributions are very different

## Key Takeaways
1. Choose collaborators for professional compatibility, not social compatibility
2. Define roles, workload, money, and creative rights BEFORE starting
3. Complementary skills create the best collaborative partnerships
4. Work style matching matters more than musical taste matching
5. Collaboration is a learning opportunity — different approaches expand your own
