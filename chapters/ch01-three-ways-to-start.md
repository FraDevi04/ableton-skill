# Chapter 1: Three Ways to Start

## Core Idea
When facing a blank DAW project with no ideas, choose one of three concrete starting points instead of being paralyzed by infinite options.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Start with the foundation**: Begin with low-pitched/rhythmic elements (bass, drums) that anchor the music. Drums provide timekeeping; bass defines chords. In experimental genres, this might be a drone or repetitive rhythm.
- **Start with what you hear**: Use spontaneous musical ideas from your head — melodic hums, tapped rhythms. These accidental ideas are often the most interesting.
- **Start with what you know**: Use a physical instrument you're comfortable with (guitar, drums) even in electronic contexts. Your physical connection generates more organic ideas than mouse/MIDI controller alone.

## Key Concepts
- **Blank slate problem**: The most intimidating place in creative work — all options available, choosing seems impossible
- **Foundation layer**: The bottom of the musical stack (low pitch, rhythm) that everything else builds on
- **Spontaneous ideas**: Musical fragments that appear outside of active work context
- **Physical connection**: Tactile relationship to an instrument that influences musical output

## Mental Models
- Use "start with foundation" when you need structural clarity
- Use "start with what you hear" when you have genuine melodic/rhythmic ideas from outside the studio
- Use "start with what you know" when you play a physical instrument and want more organic results

## Anti-patterns
- **"Just do it!"**: Too glib for creative work where the path forward isn't obvious
- **Ignoring spontaneous ideas**: Dismissing ideas that came naturally to you
- **Forcing the wrong approach**: Using a physical instrument when you have a clear internal idea, or vice versa

## Key Takeaways
1. A blank project is paralyzing — choose a starting point deliberately rather than waiting for inspiration
2. The foundation (bass + drums) provides both conceptual and musical anchor for everything else
3. Spontaneous ideas from outside the studio are valuable and should be captured
4. Physical instruments generate different, often more organic ideas than digital controllers
5. The trick is translating acoustic ideas to electronic medium — a good problem to have
