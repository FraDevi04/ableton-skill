# Chapter 41: Tuning Everything

## Core Idea
Electronic drums often have definite pitches. Tune kick drums to the song key. Tune toms in intervals spelling the root chord. Use spectrum analyzer + EQ to identify and fix out-of-tune percussion.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Electronic drum tuning**: Many electronic drums have definite pitches — tune kick drums to the song key, tune toms in intervals that spell the root chord.
- **Spectrum analysis**: Use a spectrum analyzer to identify the pitch of percussion sounds. Then use EQ to adjust or replace out-of-tune frequencies.
- **Percussive pitch**: Every percussion sound has a pitch — even if it's not obvious. Identify and tune it.

## Key Concepts
- **All percussion has pitch**: Even "unpitched" percussion has a dominant frequency
- **Tuning to the key**: Kick drums tuned to the song key create harmonic coherence
- **Tom intervals**: Toms tuned in intervals (3rd, 5th, octave) create harmonic relationships
- **EQ as tuning**: EQ can be used to emphasize or suppress the dominant frequency of a percussion sound

## Mental Models
- Use tuning thinking when your low-end feels "off" or muddy
- Think of every percussion sound as having a pitch — identify and tune it
- Spectrum analyzers are your best friend for identifying drum pitches

## Anti-patterns
- **Ignoring drum pitch**: Assuming drums are "unpitched" when they actually have a dominant frequency
- **Tuning to the wrong key**: Tuning drums to a key that doesn't match the song
- **Over-tuning**: Tuning every drum to perfection can make beats feel sterile

## Key Takeaways
1. Electronic drums often have definite pitches — tune them to the song key
2. Tune kick drums to the song root; tune toms in intervals spelling the root chord
3. Use spectrum analyzer + EQ to identify and fix out-of-tune percussion
4. Every percussion sound has a dominant frequency — identify it
5. Tuning drums creates harmonic coherence in the low-end
