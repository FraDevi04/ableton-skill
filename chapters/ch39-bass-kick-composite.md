# Chapter 39: Bass Lines and Kick Drums as a Single Composite

## Core Idea
Don't treat kick and bass as separate lines — compose them as one monophonic composite. Bass notes must fit between kick notes. Zero overlap of attacks.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Monophonic composite**: Kick and bass form a single monophonic line. Bass notes must fit BETWEEN kick notes. They share the same rhythmic space.
- **Zero overlap of attacks**: Bass and kick attacks must never overlap. Sustained bass notes are OK if their onsets don't overlap with kick onsets.
- **Low-end relationship**: The kick+bass relationship is the most important low-end decision in electronic music.

## Key Concepts
- **One line, two sounds**: Kick and bass are two sounds playing one line
- **Fitting between**: Bass notes fit in the gaps between kick notes
- **Attack vs sustain**: Attack overlap is forbidden; sustain overlap is fine
- **Genre variation**: In some genres (house, techno), kick and bass are tightly interlocked. In others (ambient), they may be more independent.

## Mental Models
- Use the monophonic composite model when programming kick and bass
- Think of kick and bass as two voices singing one melody
- The relationship between kick and bass defines the low-end

## Anti-patterns
- **Overlap attacks**: Kick and bass attacks overlapping creates muddy low-end
- **Treating them separately**: Programming kick and bass as independent lines creates conflicts
- **Ignoring the relationship**: Not considering how kick and bass interact

## Key Takeaways
1. Treat kick and bass as a single monophonic composite line
2. Bass notes must fit BETWEEN kick notes — zero overlap of attacks
3. Sustained bass notes are OK if their onsets don't overlap with kick onsets
4. The kick+bass relationship is the most important low-end decision
5. In some genres they're tightly interlocked; in others, more independent
